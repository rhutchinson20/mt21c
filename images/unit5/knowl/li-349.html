Item 2.

Alterations (maj, \(\left.\text{}{\Delta}\right.\), ♯, ♭, \(\left.\text{}{+}\right.\), m, –) are applied as follows:

  1. “maj” or “\(\left.\text{}{\Delta}\right.\)” followed by 7, 9, 11, or 13 means the 7th of the chord is major, not the 9th, 11th, or 13th.

  2. Lowercase “m” or the minus sign (“–”) means minor and applies to the 3rd of the chord.

    1. The minus sign (–) is equivalent to a flat when placed in front of 9, 11, or 13 (i.e., ♭9 and –9 mean the same thing). “\(\left.\text{C}^♯{}\right.\)–\(\left.\text{}^{6}\right.\)” (equivalent to “\(\left.\text{C}^♯{}\text{m}^{6}\right.\)”) means a \(\left.\text{C}^♯{}\right.\) minor chord (“\(\left.\text{C}^♯{}\right.\)–”) with the 6th from major (A♯). “\(\left.\text{C}^♯{}\right.\) (add –6),” which is equivalent to “\(\left.\text{C}^♯{}\left(\text{add ♭6}\right)\right.\),” means a C♯ major triad with the ♭6 added

  3. The plus sign (\(\left.\text{}{+}\right.\)) can mean “augmented triad” or is equivalent to a sharp when placed in front of 9, 11, or 13. C\(\left.\text{}{+}\right.\)7 means a C augmented triad (“C\(\left.\text{}{+}\right.\)”) with a minor seventh from the root to the seventh(“7”), not a C major triad with a major 7th.

  4. Chromatically altered chord numbers (affecting 6, 9, 11, 13) are usually set apart by parentheses so as to clearly designate whether the minus, plus, or sharp, or flat is affecting the chord or the added note. For example, consider C♭9 versus C(♭9) versus C7(♭9).
